Persistent hotspots: why some areas never change

Crime is often described as something that fluctuates over time, rising, falling, shifting across the city. But what if some places never really change? What if, beneath these fluctuations, there are persistent patterns that remain stable year after year?

To explore this idea, we focus on assault in San Francisco, one of the most relevant and impactful crime categories. Unlike rarer crimes, assault directly affects people’s safety and how they experience the city.

As shown in the figure below, assault remains consistently present over time, making it a suitable case for studying long-term spatial patterns. The crimes in the figure are ordered from lowest to highest number of incidents, which makes it easier to compare them.

In this context, assault stands out as one of the most frequent crimes, second only to larceny/theft. This combination of high frequency and consistency makes it especially useful for understanding how crime is distributed across the city.

📊 Crime Trends

Where do assaults happen?

While crime levels change over time, this does not necessarily mean that where crime happens also changes.

To explore this, we now move from time trends to geography. The map below shows how assault incidents are distributed across San Francisco, evolving year by year. Areas in red indicate a higher concentration of incidents.

🗺️ Crime Map

Looking at the map, it may seem that crime intensity changes over time. Some years appear more concentrated, while others seem more dispersed across the city.

However, a closer look reveals a much more stable pattern. The same areas, particularly in the central and eastern parts of the city, remain consistently intense throughout the entire period. While the number of incidents fluctuates, their location does not change significantly. This means that assault is not randomly distributed across the city. Instead, it keeps occurring in the same places over time.

These areas are also among the most densely populated and socially active parts of the city, with higher levels of commercial activity, nightlife, and public interaction. In contrast, western districts tend to be more residential and less dense. This difference in urban structure may help explain why incidents are more concentrated in certain areas, without necessarily implying that they are inherently more “dangerous”.

This raises some important questions. Are these areas truly more dangerous, or are they simply more visible in the data? For example, there may be more people, more police presence, or higher reporting rates in these areas. What we see as “hotspots” may not only reflect where crime happens, but also where it is more likely to be recorded.

At the same time, the persistence of these patterns over more than two decades invites further reflection. If the same areas continue to show higher levels of crime year after year, it suggests that these patterns are not temporary, but structural. Whether this is due to urban dynamics, social conditions, or institutional factors is something that cannot be fully answered with this dataset alone, but we can still observe that crime does not move as much as we might expect. Instead, some areas remain consistently affected over time, a finding that leaves open important questions about why certain parts of the city never seem to change.

When do assaults happen?

While the previous map showed where assaults tend to persist, it does not tell us when they occur. To better understand these patterns, we now zoom in on the temporal dimension within these same hotspots.

Use the interactive chart below to explore how assault incidents vary across time and between weekdays and weekends in the different districts. Weekdays are shown in blue, while weekends are highlighted in red.

⏱️ Assault Time Distribution

Assault incidents occur throughout the day, but they are not evenly distributed. Most incidents cluster during the afternoon and evening hours.

There is also a clear difference between weekdays and weekends. Weekdays follow a more regular pattern, while weekends show a wider spread of incidents, especially later at night. This suggests that social behavior, such as nightlife, leisure activities, and increased mobility, plays an important role in shaping when assaults occur.

What is particularly surprising is that these temporal patterns remain consistent within the same hotspots identified earlier. In other words, crime is structured not only in space, but also in time.

Taken together, this shows that understanding crime requires looking beyond simple counts or locations. Persistent hotspots are not only places where crime happens repeatedly, they are also places where specific daily and weekly patterns emerge over time.

What does this tell us?

Crime is often described as something that changes constantly, moving across the city over time. However, this analysis suggests a different reality. While the number of incidents may rise or fall, the places where crime concentrates tend to remain the same.

The persistence of these hotspots over more than two decades points to deeper structural patterns within the city. At the same time, the temporal analysis shows that even within these areas, crime follows clear daily and weekly rhythms. In other words, crime is not only structured in space, but also in time.

Understanding this dual structure shifts the focus from simply reacting to changes in crime levels to asking a more important question: why do certain areas remain consistently affected? Are these patterns driven by urban activity, social conditions, or the way crime is monitored and reported?

While this dataset cannot fully answer these questions, it highlights something essential. What we observe is not just where crime happens, but also how the city itself is organized and experienced.

In that sense, persistent hotspots are not only a reflection of crime — they are a reflection of the city itself.
